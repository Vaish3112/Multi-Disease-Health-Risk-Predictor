Diabetes Risk Predictor

1. Age: Enter your age in years. Age is important because the risk of diabetes increases as we get older. For example, someone over 40 may have a higher risk than a younger person.


2. Gender: Select your gender (Male or Female). Some health risks can vary slightly between males and females.


3. BMI (Body Mass Index): BMI is a measure of body fat based on your weight and height. You can calculate it as:

BMI = weight (kg) ÷ (height (m) × height (m))

For example, if you weigh 70 kg and your height is 1.7 m, BMI = 70 ÷ (1.7 × 1.7) ≈ 24.2

A higher BMI can increase the risk of diabetes, while a healthy BMI lowers the risk.


4. Fasting Blood Sugar: This is your blood sugar level after not eating for at least 8 hours. Usually measured in mg/dL. A high fasting blood sugar may indicate prediabetes or diabetes. You can measure it at a lab or using a glucometer at home.


5. Postprandial Blood Sugar: This is your blood sugar measured 2 hours after eating. It helps see how your body handles sugar. Measured in mg/dL. You can check it using a home glucometer or at a lab.


6. Family History of Diabetes: Select “Yes” if any of your parents or siblings have diabetes. Having family members with diabetes increases your risk due to genetics and shared lifestyle habits.


7. Physical Activity Level: Choose Low, Medium, or High. Regular exercise helps control blood sugar and reduces diabetes risk. Examples:

  • Low: Mostly sitting, little to no exercise
  • Medium: Walking, light exercise 2–3 times a week
  • High: Regular exercise, sports, or active lifestyle

8. Blood Pressure: Enter your blood pressure as Systolic/Diastolic (e.g., 120/80 mmHg). High blood pressure often occurs with diabetes and increases health risks. You can measure it using a home BP monitor or at a clinic.

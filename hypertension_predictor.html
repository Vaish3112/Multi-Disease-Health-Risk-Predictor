Hypertension Risk Predictor

1. Age: Enter your age in years. Blood pressure tends to rise as we age, especially after 40.


2. Gender: Men are more likely to develop high blood pressure earlier in life, while women’s risk increases after menopause.


3. BMI (Body Mass Index): Extra body weight increases the risk of hypertension.
Formula: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ (height (m) × height (m))
Example: 70 ÷ (1.7 × 1.7) ≈ 24.2 (Normal range: 18.5–24.9).
You can calculate BMI at home using a scale and measuring tape.


4. Systolic Blood Pressure: The top number in a BP reading (e.g., 120 in 120/80). It measures the pressure when the heart beats.
How to measure: Use a home digital BP monitor or have it checked at a clinic.


5. Diastolic Blood Pressure: The bottom number in a BP reading (e.g., 80 in 120/80). It measures the pressure when the heart rests between beats.


6. Family History: If your parents or siblings have hypertension, your risk is higher due to genetics and shared lifestyle habits.


7. Physical Activity: Regular exercise keeps blood vessels healthy and reduces BP risk.

  • Low: Mostly sitting, little or no exercise.
  • Medium: Walking or light exercise 2–3 times a week.
  • High: Regular exercise, sports, or physically active lifestyle.

8. Salt Intake: High salt in food raises blood pressure.

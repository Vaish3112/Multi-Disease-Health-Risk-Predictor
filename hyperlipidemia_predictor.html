Hyperlipidemia Risk Predictor

1. Age: Cholesterol levels often rise with age, especially after 40. Older adults are more at risk.


2. Gender: Men generally develop high cholesterol earlier, while women’s risk rises after menopause due to lower estrogen levels.


3. BMI (Body Mass Index): Extra body weight is linked to higher LDL and triglycerides.
Formula: BMI = weight (kg) ÷ (height (m) × height (m))
Example: If weight = 80 kg and height = 1.6 m → BMI = 80 ÷ (1.6 × 1.6) = 31.2 (Obese).
Overweight/obese individuals are more likely to have high cholesterol.


4. Total Cholesterol: The total amount of cholesterol in your blood.
Normal: Below 200 mg/dL.
How to measure: A blood test (lipid profile) at a lab.


5. LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein): Known as “bad cholesterol.” High LDL causes fatty deposits in arteries.
Optimal: Less than 100 mg/dL.


6. HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein): Known as “good cholesterol.” It removes bad cholesterol from arteries.
Good: Above 40 mg/dL (men), 50 mg/dL (women).


7. Triglycerides: A type of fat in the blood. High levels increase heart disease risk.
Normal: Less than 150 mg/dL.
How to measure: Part of a lipid profile blood test.


8. Family History: If your parents or siblings have high cholesterol or early heart disease (before age 55 in men, 65 in women), you may be at higher risk.
